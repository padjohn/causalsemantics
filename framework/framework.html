Framework

Conceptual foundations of Causal Semantics (S_C)

Logic, Language & Computation

Causal Semantics integrates insights from three research areas that have largely operated independently:

1. Philosophical Causal Theories

Philosophical accounts differentiate between:

  • Monocausal structures:
    • A cause is both sufficient and necessary
  • Polycausal structures:
    • A cause can be sufficient
      • Where there’s fire, there is smoke.
      • If, when
    • A cause can be necessary
      • No smoke without a fire.
      • If and only if, only

Yet these theories remain abstract and haven’t been operationalized for natural language analysis.

Key references: [1], [2], [3], [4]

2. Linguistic Semantics

Linguistic analyses identify crucial semantic dimensions:

  • Polarity: Promoting or inhibiting influences [5]
  • Modality: Factive or hypothetical causation [6]
  • Discourse functions: Causal attributions as argumentative tool [7]

Yet these studies remain limited to small, qualitatively analyzed corpora.

3. Computational Causal Extraction

NLP systems scale to large text collections but typically:

  • Reduce causality to binary Cause-Effect pairs
  • Ignore semantic nuances like strength or direction
  • Lack discourse sensitivity

Key references: [8], [9], [10]

The Integration

Causal Semantics bridges these traditions by:

  1. Formalizing philosophical distinctions (mono- vs. polycausal) as quantifiable salience values
  2. Enriching computational extraction with semantic dimensions (polarity, salience)
  3. Scaling linguistic insights through automated extraction pipelines

The result is a framework that is:

  • Semantically enriched: Captures nuanced attributions
  • Computationally tractable: Scales to millions of sentences

(C, E, I) Cause, Effect & Influence

The framework models causal relations as triples:

R = (C, E, I) where:

  • C (Cause): The causing entity
  • E (Effect): The affected entity
  • I (Influence): A signed scalar \in [-1, +1] encoding:
    • \pm (Polarity) : promoting (+) or inhibiting (−)
    • ||I|| (Salience): monocausal (1.0) or polycausal (<1.0)

See: Understanding (C,E,I) Tuples for detailed explanation.

Two Core Dimensions

Polarity: Direction

Does the cause promote or inhibit the effect?

  • Promoting (+): C increases the probability/intensity of E
    • Climate change causes species extinction
  • Inhibiting (−): C decreases the probability/intensity of E
    • Conservation prevents species extinction

Linguistically realized through:

  • Antonym verb pairs (increase/reduce, cause/prevent)
  • Nominal indicators (cause vs. prevention)
  • Prepositional markers (for vs. against)

See: Polarity section for examples and analysis.

Salience: Magnitude

How strong is this cause relative to all factors?

  • Monocausal (|I| = 1.0): Sole or exclusive factor
    • X is the cause of Y
  • Prioritized (|I| = 0.75): Dominant but not exclusive
    • Mainly X causes Y
  • Distributed (||I|| = 0.5): One of several equal factors
    • X contributes to Y, X and Y cause Z

Linguistically realized through:

  • Determination (the vs. a cause)
  • Composition (main cause, partial cause)
  • Distribution markers (among others, also)

See: Salience section for computation methods.

From Tuples to Graphs

Individual (C, E, I) tuples aggregate into Attributional Causal Graphs (ACGs):

  • Nodes: Entities (C and E values)
  • Edges: Weighted, directed connections with I as weight
  • Properties:
    • Positive edges (I > 0): promoting factors
    • Negative edges (I < 0): inhibiting factors
    • Edge weights = aggregated evidence across multiple texts

ACGs enable:

  • Network analysis of discourse structure
  • Identification of central vs. peripheral causes
  • Temporal tracking of attribution shifts

See: ACG Theory for graph-theoretic foundations.

Applications

The framework reveals:

1. Argumentation Patterns

  • Which entities are positioned as causes vs. effects?
  • Are attributions monocausal (simplified) or polycausal (nuanced)?
  • What role do inhibiting relations play (solutions vs. problems)?

2. Temporal Dynamics

  • How do causal attributions shift over time?
  • When do new causes enter discourse?
  • When do attributions strengthen or weaken?

3. Responsibility Distribution

  • Which actors are attributed causal agency?
  • How is responsibility distributed across entities?
  • What patterns of blame or credit emerge?

## $(C, E, I)$ Cause, Effect & Influence

The framework models causal relations as triples:

$$
R = (C, E, I)
$$
where:  

* $C$ ([**Cause**]{.smallcaps}): The causing entity  
* $E$ ([**Effect**]{.smallcaps}): The affected entity  
* $I$ ([**Influence**]{.smallcaps}): A signed scalar $\in [-1, +1]$ encoding:  
    + $\pm$ ([**Polarity**]{.smallcaps}) : promoting ($+$) or inhibiting ($−$)  
    + $||I||$ ([**Salience**]{.smallcaps}): monocausal ($1.0$) or polycausal ($<1.0$)

**See**: [Understanding (C,E,I) Tuples](tuples.qmd) for detailed explanation.

## Two Core Dimensions

### Polarity: Direction

Does the cause promote or inhibit the effect?

- **Promoting** ($+$): $C$ increases the probability/intensity of $E$
  - *Climate change causes species extinction*
- **Inhibiting** ($−$): $C$ decreases the probability/intensity of $E$
  - *Conservation prevents species extinction*

Linguistically realized through:

- Antonym verb pairs (*increase/reduce*, *cause/prevent*)
- Nominal indicators (*cause* vs. *prevention*)
- Prepositional markers (*for* vs. *against*)

**See**: [Polarity section](tuples.qmd#sec-polarity) for examples and analysis.

### Salience: Magnitude

How strong is this cause relative to all factors?

- **Monocausal** ($|I| = 1.0$): Sole or exclusive factor
  - *X is the cause of Y*
- **Prioritized** ($|I| = 0.75$): Dominant but not exclusive
  - *Mainly X causes Y*
- **Distributed** ($||I|| = 0.5$): One of several equal factors
  - *X contributes to Y*, *X and Y cause Z*

Linguistically realized through:

- Determination (*the* vs. *a* cause)
- Composition (*main cause*, *partial cause*)
- Distribution markers (*among others*, *also*)

**See**: [Salience section](tuples.qmd#sec-salience) for computation methods.

## From Tuples to Graphs

Individual $(C, E, I)$ tuples aggregate into **Attributional Causal Graphs** (ACGs):

- **Nodes**: Entities ($C$ and $E$ values)
- **Edges**: Weighted, directed connections with $I$ as weight
- **Properties**: 
  - Positive edges ($I > 0$): promoting factors
  - Negative edges ($I < 0$): inhibiting factors
  - Edge weights = aggregated evidence across multiple texts

ACGs enable:

- Network analysis of discourse structure
- Identification of central vs. peripheral causes
- Temporal tracking of attribution shifts

**See**: [ACG Theory](acg-theory.qmd) for graph-theoretic foundations.
